Planned Happenstance

I've got a simple question: "How many of you think your current academic path was completely planned from the beginning?"

* What events shaped where you are now? Expected and unexpected?
* What about people you met, mentors who helped you, classes you took, experiences you'd had?

(Are there any patterns here?)

We're scientists and like to think about the world rationally, but there are so many complex pieces that we can't really have a clear master plan, only trajectories – vectors we can follow.

Planned Happenstance
Planned Happenstance is the idea that chance events play a major role in careers and intellectual trajectories, but that we can act in ways that increase the likelihood of beneficial chance events and prepare ourselves to recognize and use them when they occur.

This is based on a paper by Mitchell, Levin, and Krumboltz, Planned Happenstance: Constructing Unexpected Career Opportunities, Journal of Counseling & Development, 1999. Formally, they introduce this way:

Humans are born with differing characteristics and predispositions at a given time and place to parents not of their own choosing. They grow up in an environment where innumerable unpredictable events occur that provide opportunities for learning of both a positive and negative nature. Individuals can also generate events on their own and can capitalize on all kinds of events and resources to maximize their learning. The counselors job is to facilitate the learning of skills, interests, beliefs, values, work habits and personal qualities that enable each client to create a satisfying life in a constantly changing work environment. Planned happenstance theory is a conceptual framework extending career counseling to include the creating and transforming of unplanned events into opportunities for learning. The goal of a planned happenstance intervention is to assist clients to generate, recognize, and incorporate chance events into their career development. The words planned happenstance have been intentionally united as an oxymoron. Clients must plan to generate and be receptive to chance opportunities. A strong component of planned happenstance is facilitating the clients actions of generating and anticipating possible opportunities. Planned happenstance theory should not be confused with magical thinking or reliance on fate. Clients should not merely meander through experiences initiated by others while passively awaiting a knock on the door. They need  to learn to take action to generate and find opportunities.

* The goal is not to eliminate uncertainty
* The goal is to become skilled at navigating and generating opportunity within uncertainty!

Think about cultivating:
* Curiosity
* Persistence
* Flexibility
* Optimism
* Risk-taking


As I mentioned at the beginning of this quarter, in my science communication career, my opportunities rarely arrived through formal channels alone. They often emerged through side conversations at events, small risks, unfinished ideas, and chance meetings.

How do scientists typically get invited to give public talks, write for public venues, or engage broader audiences?

My guesses as to what they'll say:

* They're famous
* Someone asked them
* A connection
* A mentor suggested them

But emphasize that so much happens through
* Weak ties
* Informal networks
* Visible curiosity
* Small acts of risk-taking
* Being known as someone who cares about communicating

Not through:

* (Just) formal applications
* Waiting until you feel "ready"
* Perfect long-term planning

Strong ties are the people who know you well: your labmates, advisors, close collaborators, and friends.

Weak ties are the people who know of you but don’t interact with you regularly — acquaintances, former colleagues, people you’ve met once or twice, or friends-of-friends.

My website has a "Lab Philosophy" page. In one section, I say that scientists need to learn to network. Talk to other researchers. Email people about their work when you have questions. Don’t be shy. Or rather, go ahead and be shy but recognize that lots of people are shy and the only way to learn from them is to overcome your mutual shyness. (This advice isn’t meant as a Machiavellian ploy; networking allows you to meet smart people, which gives you new ideas and new collaborations. This, in turn, lets you do science faster and better. Networking is sharing, not manipulating.) If you can’t network in person, do so online!

So, many PhD students dislike “networking.”

Reframe it:

This exercise is not about transactional networking.
It is about becoming a group that helps one another:

* Notice opportunities
* Create opportunities
* Encourage risk-taking
* Amplify one another’s work

Together you *are* a network!
